About

I am a final-year PhD candidate at QUT's Australian Cobotics Centre and Construction & Architecture Robotics Lab (CARL). My research develops sociotechnical accounts of human-robot collaboration — examining how surgical teams coordinate with robotic systems in Mako-assisted orthopaedic surgery, and how construction workers integrate robotic tools into their practice.

My methodological home is Socio-Technical Grounded Theory (STGT), combined with ethnographic fieldwork, video analysis, and expert interviews. My doctoral thesis proposes the 3T Framework for HRC Teamwork — a theoretical contribution structured around Team, Tempo, and Task dimensions, with Accumulated Experience as a cross-cutting condition.

Before coming to QUT, I completed an M.A. in Media-Human Interaction and a B.Sc. in Communication and Media at Erasmus University Rotterdam, where I also held a teaching position.

Research themes

HRC in robotic surgery

Ethnographic study of Mako-assisted orthopaedic surgery — examining how surgeons, scrub nurses, and Stryker specialists co-constitute robotic practice in real operating theatres.

HRC in construction & AEC

Developing the cHRCAM (construction HRC Acceptance Model) — a four-layer sociotechnical framework explaining how construction teams accept and adapt to robotic co-workers.

Multi-actor HRC (mHRC)

A PRISMA scoping review of 34 studies proposing a functional role typology: primary operator, technical mediator, procedural support, and supervisor/monitor.

Theory & methodology

Advancing STGT for qualitative HRI research, including its first application to video-based data analysis and the development of the 3T Framework as a transferable theoretical contribution.
