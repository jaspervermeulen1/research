How do teams work with and around robots?

I study how people coordinate around robotic systems in real-world environments. Through embedded qualitative research, I investigate the gap between how human-robot collaboration is designed and how it unfolds in practice.

I am a final-year PhD candidate at the Queensland University of Technology (QUT) and the Australian Cobotics Centre. My research investigates human-robot collaboration in high-stakes environments, with a particular focus on robotic surgery and multi-actor teamwork around robotic systems.

Using ethnographic fieldwork, interviews, and video-based analysis, I examine how robotic systems reshape coordination, communication, expertise, and human work in practice. My doctoral research draws on more than 100 hours of fieldwork across observations, video analysis, and interviews conducted across Australian surgical contexts.

My methodological foundation is Socio-Technical Grounded Theory (STGT). My thesis introduces the 3T Framework for HRC Teamwork, a transferable sociotechnical framework structured around Team, Tempo, and Task dimensions.

Before joining QUT, I completed degrees in Human-Media Interaction and Communication & Media at Erasmus University Rotterdam, where I also taught qualitative methods and emerging media technologies.

Research themes

HRC in robot-assisted surgery

Ethnographic research examining how surgeons, nurses, and robotic specialists coordinate robotic orthopaedic surgery in real operating theatres.

HRC in construction and AEC

Developing sociotechnical frameworks for construction human-robot collaboration, including models of robotic acceptance and adoption in AEC environments.

Multi-actor HRC

Investigating how multiple humans collaborate around robotic systems through role typologies, coordination structures, and distributed teamwork models.

Theory and methodology

Advancing qualitative and sociotechnical methods for HRI research, including STGT applications for individual data analysis and theory generation.
