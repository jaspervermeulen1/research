Research

Human-robot collaboration in the wild

My research examines how robotic systems become workable in real settings, especially where collaboration depends on many people, roles, tools, spatial arrangements, and organisational routines.

Doctoral research programme

My PhD investigates human-centred and multi-actor human-robot collaboration through the case of robot-assisted orthopaedic surgery. Rather than treating collaboration as a simple relationship between one human and one robot, the project examines how robotic surgery is coordinated across surgeons, nurses, assistants, Mako Product Specialists, instruments, displays, and operating theatre constraints.

The project uses Socio-Technical Grounded Theory, combining video-based analysis, ethnographic observation, and expert interviews to develop a grounded account of how human-robot collaboration is sustained in practice.

Research themes

Robot-assisted surgery

Fieldwork in orthopaedic operating theatres, focused on coordination, expertise, spatial work, and the human labour that supports Makoplasty procedures.

Multi-actor HRC

Theory and design implications for collaboration that involves many human actors, not only a single human and robot dyad.

Socio-technical methodology

Applications of Socio-Technical Grounded Theory for qualitative HRI, video analysis, fieldwork, and theory generation.

AEC robotics

Human-robot collaboration and adoption in construction and architecture, engineering, and construction contexts.
