Research

Human-robot collaboration in the wild

My research studies how robotic systems become workable through human coordination, expertise, repair, and adaptation in real organisational settings.

Doctoral research

My PhD examines human-robot collaboration in robot-assisted orthopaedic surgery, focusing on Mako-assisted arthroplasty. Rather than treating collaboration as a dyad between one surgeon and one robot, the research examines how surgery is coordinated across surgeons, nurses, assistants, Mako Product Specialists, instruments, displays, spatial arrangements, and institutional routines.

The project asks how human-centred collaboration with robots is enacted in practice and how empirical insights from surgical work can inform the theorisation and design of multi-actor human-robot collaboration.

Research themes

HRC in robot-assisted surgery

Ethnographic research examining how surgeons, nurses, assistants, and robotic specialists coordinate robotic orthopaedic surgery in real operating theatres.

Multi-actor HRC

Theory and design work on collaboration around robots where multiple human roles contribute to, repair, and sustain the work.

Invisible and anticipatory work

Studies of support, repair, translation, and anticipation that often remain invisible in technical accounts of robotic performance.

AEC and construction robotics

Socio-technical frameworks for human-robot collaboration in construction and AEC contexts, including adoption, acceptance, and role distribution.

Socio-Technical Grounded Theory

Methodological development of STGT for individual qualitative analysis, video-based HRI research, and theory generation.

Human factors and work systems

Analysis of how cognition, embodiment, spatial organisation, communication, and workload are distributed across human-robot work systems.

Current direction

The next stage of this programme develops a socio-technical grounded theory of multi-actor HRC. The theory explains how collaboration with robots is held together through role distribution, delegated responsibility, invisible support work, and situated coordination across people and artefacts.
